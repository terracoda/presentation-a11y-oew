So we have some awareness

Let's start making content accessible one step at time. We need to employ special techniques to build-in the blue part - to turn the stairs into a ramp.

Building the virtual ramp

Our goal in WCAG 2.0 language is to make our web content:

  1. Perceivable

  2. Operable

  3. Understandable

  4. Robust

(WCAG 2.0, 2008)

The access techniques that I will now describe focus on making web content accessible one media type at a time – one step at a time.

I'm intentionally not talking about exact WCAG success criteria – our goal is accessibility not just compliance. Each step may correspond to more than one success criteria.

A person is not going to sue you because you failed on a particular success criterion, but because he/she could not access something really important on your website (likely multiple times).

Most people who have access problems don’t sue. Most people just have a bad experience and never come back.

2. Text

Accessible by nature, but fully accessible text-based content needs:

  1. A declared human language.

    <html lang="en"> The browser needs to know what language the content is written in, and it needs to made aware of language changes, for example <q lang="fr">Bonjour!</q>

  2. Semantic content hierarchy

    sections, headings and marked divisions.

  3. Semantic content structure

    headings, paragraphs, lists, quotations, etc.

Colour & Layout

We need to choose colours wisely to avoid easily confused colours.

  • Don't rely on color alone for meaning.
  • Test for sufficient contrast.
  • Use CSS for design and layout – not tables.
  • We need to build some awareness around common color issues caused by different kinds of colour blindness.
  • We need to use icons or other formatting – not just color – to communicate important things such as links or errors messages.

Tables &Forms

Keep it simple

  • Is this truly tabular data? Is it easy to read?
  • Do we need that bit of user data?
  • Forms & tables have a rich set of mark-up to make them accessible.
  • Tables need a captions. Form controls need labels.

Forms & complex data tables have often been the basis for lawsuits.
